Research Study on Methamphetamine (METH) and Brain Health

Overview

Methamphetamine (METH) can seriously damage the brain, leading to memory problems, depression, heart issues, and other health problems that can persist even after stopping METH use. This damage can make recovery more difficult. We also know that METH can mess with the immune system and kill brain cells, but we don't fully understand if brain inflammation during recovery plays a big role in these issues. Additionally, weâ€™re exploring if certain genes affect how the brain responds to METH and its withdrawal.

Our Study

We found that long-term exposure to METH increases the activity of a gene that controls calcium entry into brain cells. Too much calcium is harmful to the cells. People with certain genetic variants of this gene might be at higher risk for brain disorders and inflammation-related issues when using METH.

Our Goals

1. Understand Genetics and Inflammation: We want to understand how genetic factors influence inflammation in the brain and body during METH use and withdrawal.

2. Investigate Chronic Inflammation: We aim to see how long-term inflammation affects brain function and thinking skills in METH users.

Hypothesis

We believe that people with a specific genetic makeup (A/A genotype) will have higher levels of inflammation markers in their brain and blood when using METH, compared to those without these genetic markers.

Research Aims

1. Brain Chemicals: Compare levels of certain brain chemicals (GABA, Glu, mI, NAA) in METH users with different genetic types and non-users.

2. Inflammation Markers: Compare blood levels of inflammation markers in METH users with different genetic types and non-users, and study changes in DNA related to inflammation.
